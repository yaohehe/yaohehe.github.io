I've spent the past month testing three Amazon Basics LED desk lamps — the 7W compact, 9W eye-care, and 12W long-arm models. For context, I'm a graphic designer who works from a home office, so "works for reading and detailed tasks" is my actual bar, not marketing language.

Bottom line up front:

  • **On a tight budget, mostly paperwork** → 7W classic (~$9.99). Gets the job done, best value
  • **Designer or coder, long hours at screen** → 9W eye-care (~$14.99). Adjustable color temperature is a genuine need, not a luxury
  • **Large desk, need to illuminate a wide area** → 12W long-arm (~$19.99), but mind the footprint

7W Classic: Functional, But Sets Low Expectations

The 7W is the first lamp I bought. The power constraint shows. Fixed 4000K warm white color temperature, measured brightness around 300lux at 30cm — fine for reading a document, but pushing it if you want to reference a book on the side while working.

Build quality matches the price. Metal-weighted base does the job, though the lamp head has noticeable plastic feel. The USB-A charging port on the side of the base works, but the cable sticks out awkwardly — not the clean look if cable management matters to you.

Good for: Students, under $10 budget, cramped desk spaces.

Not for: Anyone needing serious brightness or precision eye work for extended periods.

9W Eye-Care: The One I'd Actually Buy Again

If 7W is "functional," 9W is "actually good." Three color temperature modes (3000K warm / 4000K neutral / 6000K cool white), measured max brightness 600lux — at 40cm I could comfortably read a gloss-coated magazine without straining.

The detail I appreciate most: the lamp head adjusts with just-right resistance. With my previous lamp, every angle adjustment meant holding the head with one hand while repositioning. The 9W's damping is tuned well — single-handed adjustment without the head sliding out of position mid-adjustment.

Memory function is genuinely useful. Power cuts and restores? It resumes your last brightness setting automatically. As someone who regularly writes until 2am, not having to re-adjust every time I hit the light switch is a small but real quality-of-life improvement.

USB-C port on the rear of the base — cable routes under the base for clean desk setup. Base is slightly heavier than the 7W model, which translates to noticeably better stability when you bump the desk.

Good for: Designers, programmers, anyone at a desk for 4+ hours daily.

Not for: Users needing to illuminate beyond 60cm from the base — brightness drops significantly at range.

12W Long-Arm: Only Makes Sense for Large Desks

The 12W's defining feature is arm reach — lamp head extends up to 60cm from the base. For L-shaped desks or anyone needing to light a wide work area, this reach actually matters.

The tradeoff: base diameter is 23cm, which ate half my mouse pad. The arm joints are plastic with metal reinforcement — stable initially, but after months of regular adjustment the damping noticeably loosens.

Another issue: color temperature has only two modes (4000K/6000K), missing the 9W's 3000K warm light option. Late night writing in winter, 4000K still feels cold.

Overall assessment: viable if you have a large desk and need wide coverage; otherwise the 9W is the more practical choice.

Decision Framework

Before buying, ask yourself three things:

How big is your desk? Small 30cm corner desk — 7W works fine. Standard 50cm depth office desk — 9W is right. L-shaped or larger — 12W's reach actually helps.

What's your primary work? Simple document work — 7W enough. If you're at a screen for 4+ hours a day — the eye-care features justify the extra $5.

What's your ambient lighting like? If you have overhead lights and just need task lighting — 7W fine. If your desk lamp is the main light source — 9W's brightness is the safer bet.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

"Higher wattage is always better" — not really. More power means more heat and energy draw. The 12W model hit 42°C on the lamp head after 2 hours of continuous use; the 9W stayed around 36°C. Fine with AC in summer, borderline in a heated winter office.

"Buy the brightest available" — 600lux versus 800lux measured brightness is barely perceptible at normal desk distances. What actually affects day-to-day use is adjustable color temperature and even light distribution, where the 9W actually outperforms the 12W.
