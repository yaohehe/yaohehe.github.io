Glass Containers: Detailed Performance

What makes glass the better choice for reheating

Borosilicate glass tolerates thermal shock better than regular glass — meaning you can take a container straight from the freezer to the microwave without the sudden temperature change causing cracks. That's not a small thing if you do weekly meal prep and freeze portions in advance.

The silicone gasket on Amazon Basics glass containers maintains its elasticity notably well after repeated wash cycles. I checked mine after 3 months of daily dishwasher use and didn't notice the degradation I'd expect from budget containers. The seal stays airtight even with liquid-heavy foods like soup or curry.

One practical note: glass containers from any brand — Amazon Basics included — will chip at the edges if they knock against each other in the dishwasher. This is normal wear. The containers remain safe to use even with minor chips, but inspect periodically if you run them through the dishwasher frequently.

Who should prioritize glass

If your household regularly reheats food directly in the container — as opposed to transferring food to a plate — glass is the clear recommendation. The thermal performance, combined with zero plastic concern and superior seal quality, justifies the $7 price premium over plastic for most households.

Families with young children benefit from the chemical stability of glass — no concern about micro-scratches on plastic releasing particles. And anyone who stores strongly aromatic foods (curry, garlic-heavy dishes, fish) will appreciate that glass doesn't retain odors the way plastic does.

The main caveats: glass is heavy. A filled glass container with several days of meal prep can weigh well over 500g. This matters if you're packing lunch for work and want to keep your bag light. And glass breaks. If your kitchen sees a lot of "container gets knocked off the counter" action, the shatter risk is real.

Plastic Containers: Detailed Performance

Where plastic actually makes sense

Tritan plastic — the material Amazon Basics uses for their plastic food storage — is a BPA-free copolyester that handles microwave and dishwasher use better than older plastic formulations. It's not the cheap LDPE plastic you'll find in bargain bin containers.

For fridge-only storage, plastic containers are genuinely convenient. They're light enough to stack stably without the top-heavy risk of glass. They nest efficiently. And they don't shatter if the fridge door closes hard on them.

The sealing performance on Amazon Basics plastic is good — not quite as airtight as glass under pressure (try shaking a full container and you'll see glass stays dry longer), but for 90% of storage scenarios it's completely adequate.

The 3-month test did reveal one consistent issue: the silicone seal on plastic containers requires more maintenance attention than glass. If you don't hand-wash and fully dry the seal groove regularly, mold buildup occurs. The dishwasher helps, but the groove needs periodic manual cleaning to stay fresh. This isn't unique to Amazon Basics — it's a universal plastic container issue — but it's worth noting.

Who should prioritize plastic

If your primary use is fridge storage and you rarely microwave directly in the container, plastic is the practical choice. The weight saving matters for lunch-box scenarios. And for households where containers get moved, stacked, and potentially dropped regularly, the shatterproof nature is a real advantage.

Outdoor and work-lunch scenarios favor plastic decisively. A container that survives a backpack commute without concern about cracking is genuinely more useful than a technically superior but fragile glass option.

The $7 difference between a 5-piece plastic set ($15-$19) and a glass set ($22-$26) isn't trivial in absolute terms, but it's small relative to what you're committing to in a kitchen purchase. But if your use case genuinely centers on fridge storage, transport, and meal prep without regular microwave reheating, plastic delivers adequate performance at a lower entry point.
