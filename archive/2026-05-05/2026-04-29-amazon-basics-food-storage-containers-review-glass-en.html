Amazon Basics Food Storage Containers Review: Glass vs Plastic – Which One Actually Works

TL;DR: Go glass if you microwave daily (safer, no odors, lasts longer). Go plastic if you mostly refrigerate or need something portable (lighter, cheaper, won't shatter). Neither belongs in the oven.

Real usage observations:

The Snap Lock lids actually work. I tested with water stored horizontally for 24 hours – no leaks. Glass doesn't retain food odors either. Tomato-based sauces that would haunt plastic containers for days wipe clean without residue.

The main advantage is health safety. No plasticizers, no BPA, no concerns about chemical migration when reheating high-fat foods. Drawbacks: Heavy. Will shatter if dropped on tile or concrete. Lid gasket eventually needs replacing (usually after 1-2 years of heavy use, but replacement lids are inexpensive on Amazon).

Plastic Containers – Amazon Basics

The material story:

Amazon Basics uses Tritan copolyester in most current models. Tritan (from Eastman) is BPA-free, optically clear, and handles temperatures up to about 205°F. Older lines use standard PP plastic, also BPA-free but less heat-tolerant. Both are considered safe, but the temperature limits matter in practice.

Common configurations:

  • 10-piece assorted set: ~$20
  • 20-piece value pack: ~$25
  • Individual small container: $3-5

Real usage observations:

Portability is the standout feature. Same capacity, plastic weighs about 1/3 of glass. Works well for office lunch bags, picnics, or kids' meals. I took the 20-piece value pack on a camping trip and the weight difference was immediately obvious compared to the glass options.

The Snap Lock mechanism on Amazon Basics plastic containers is generally reliable when new. Long-term durability varies – some users report lid elasticity degrading after 6-12 months of heavy dishwasher use, which affects the seal. I've noticed the same pattern with the sets I've tested. The fix is simple: replace the lids, which cost less than $5 per lid on Amazon.

Caution: Even when labeled microwave-safe, stay under 212°F. High-oil or high-sugar foods can exceed the safe temperature threshold and cause warping. I've seen this happen with olive oil-based sauces reheated on full power – the container deformed slightly, though it remained functional.

Who Should Buy Which

Choose glass if you:

  • Reheat meals in the microwave most days
  • Prioritize eliminating plastic from food contact
  • Have a stable home kitchen (no kids constantly dropping things)
  • Want a one-time purchase that lasts 10+ years with proper care

Choose plastic if you:

  • Mainly use containers for refrigeration, not reheating
  • Pack lunches for work, school, or outdoor activities
  • Want maximum variety for minimum budget
  • Have young children where breakage risk is high

Neither is right for:

  • Conventional oven cooking (pushing the 400°F limit is risky with the lid on)
  • Direct flame (no food container is designed for this)
