Section 1: CADR Performance — What the Numbers Actually Mean

CADR (Clean Air Delivery Rate) tells you how fast a purifier can clean air. Higher is better, but context matters.

Large Room Model (580 m³/h):

In my 450 sq ft home office, I ran the large room model at max for 15 minutes with an air quality monitor. PM2.5 dropped from 85 to under 30 — roughly a 65% improvement in a quarter hour. That's genuinely impressive. For an open-plan living room of 2,000+ sq ft, this unit can cycle the air effectively in under an hour on turbo mode.

Bedroom Model (238 m³/h):

In my 160 sq ft bedroom, the unit ran at medium-high and brought PM2.5 from 55 to under 25 in about 20 minutes. More than adequate for a space this size. The key difference is that the bedroom model is designed to sustain air quality at low noise — it doesn't need to be a "fast blast" machine.

The catch: The large room model's high CADR comes with proportionally higher noise. At max setting, I measured ~52 dB — comparable to a running dishwasher. Great for when you're out, not great for nighttime.

Section 2: Filtration System — Both Are 3-Stage, But Not Equal

Both models advertise 3-stage filtration:

1. Pre-filter: Captures large particles (dust, pet hair)

2. HEPA filter: Traps 99.97% of particles ≥0.3 microns (pollen, mold spores, dust mites)

3. Activated carbon filter: Absorbs odors, VOCs, pet smell, cooking fumes

The key difference is carbon layer thickness. The bedroom model (B0CL993435) uses a thicker activated carbon layer specifically marketed for odor and VOC removal. In my tests, the bedroom model handled cooking odors and pet smells noticeably faster — within 30 minutes of a curry cooking session, the smell was gone. The large room model took closer to 50 minutes.

Both HEPA filters are the true-HEPA type, which is what you want for allergy and asthma sufferers. Don't settle for "HEPA-type" or "HEPA-style" — those are marketing terms with much lower filtration standards.

FAQ

Q: Can air purifiers remove COVID-19 particles?

A: True-HEPA filters (both Amazon Basics models use them) can capture virus-laden droplets from the air. However, air purifiers are a supplementary measure — they don't replace ventilation, masking, or vaccination.

Q: Can I use the large room model in my bedroom?

A: Technically yes, but the noise at higher settings makes it a poor choice for nighttime. If you must, run it on the lowest setting during the day and turn it off at night.

Q: How often should I replace filters?

A: Amazon recommends every 6-12 months. If you live in a high-pollution area or have pets, err on the side of 6 months. If you notice any smell returning or reduced airflow, replace sooner.
